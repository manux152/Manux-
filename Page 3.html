About Machu Picchu

Nestled high in the Andes Mountains of Peru, Machu Picchu is an ancient Incan city that has captivated the world with its breathtaking beauty and mysterious history. Often referred to as the "Lost City of the Incas," Machu Picchu is a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.

Each wonder featured here is a testament to human ingenuity and the rich tapestry of cultures that have shaped our world. From the towering Great Wall of China to the mystical Machu Picchu, each site has its own unique story waiting to be discovered.

"A marvel of ancient engineering and a testament to the ingenuity of the Inca civilization."

Built in the 15th century, Machu Picchu is renowned for its sophisticated dry-stone construction, terraced fields, and panoramic views of the surrounding mountains and valleys.

Historical Significance

Machu Picchu provides invaluable insights into Incan civilization, their architectural prowess, and their harmonious relationship with nature.

The site remained hidden from the outside world until its rediscovery in 1911 by Hiram Bingham, sparking global interest and archaeological study.

Visitors Received

Machu Picchu attracts over a million visitors annually, drawn by its historical significance and breathtaking scenery.

Tourism is carefully managed to preserve the site, with daily visitor limits and guided tours.

Fun Facts

Machu Picchu is often shrouded in mist, adding to its mystical allure and earning it the nickname "The Lost City in the Clouds."

The site is aligned with astronomical events, such as the solstices, indicating the Incas' advanced understanding of astronomy.

Construction Details

Construction began in the mid-15th century under Emperor Pachacuti. Using ashlar technique, stones were cut to fit together without mortar.

Water management systems, including aqueducts and fountains, were ingeniously designed to provide fresh water throughout the site.

Architectural Features

The sites dry-stone construction has allowed many structures to withstand earthquakes.

Terraced fields demonstrate advanced agricultural practices adapted to mountainous terrain.

The Intihuatana stone, a ritual stone associated with astronomy, is a notable feature of Machu Picchu.

Cultural Impact

Machu Picchu has become a symbol of Incan heritage and pride.

It has inspired countless artists, writers, and travelers worldwide.
